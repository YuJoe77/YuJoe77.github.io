The Days of Art Training for the College Entrance Examination
—— Sara

The days of art training for the college entrance examination are the most unforgettable journey of my youth. Many people think that art is a shortcut to getting into college, but only those who have truly experienced it know that it is a path full of thorns.

Every day of training felt like a race against time. In the early morning, as the lights in the art studio turned on, we began our long day. The pigments on the canvas, from the morning mist to the night stars, witnessed our efforts. When inspiration dried up, the paintbrush would stall on the paper, and the anxiety in our hearts had nowhere to go. When our skills couldn’t match our vision, we would look at others’ works and feel our hands disobedient. The feeling of being unable to keep up with our own expectations was like a mountain pressing on our hearts. These were the daily routines of training.

Our friends around us were both companions and competitors. We encouraged each other and also competed with each other. Unknowingly, “involution” became the norm. The pressure of the environment, the doubts from the outside world, and those uncontrollable factors all became invisible shackles that bound us. Art itself has no standard, and beauty varies from person to person. The judges’ evaluations were even more unpredictable. Every time we submitted our work, it felt like handing over our hopes to fate, with hearts full of anxiety.

But the principal’s words were like a beam of light piercing through the darkness. The daily demonstration classes were his most relaxed moments and also the most relaxing times for us. He would always say, “The art college entrance examination is like a thousand troops and ten thousand horses crossing a narrow bridge, and everyone is giving their best effort.” When facing pressure, he would gently pat our shoulders and softly say, “If you keep something in mind, it will surely come true. As long as you have the determination, don’t think about anything else, just strive for it and keep painting.”

During those days, the lights in the art studio never went out. We wrote our dreams with our brushes and painted our hopes with pigments. The principal’s words became the lighthouse in our hearts, illuminating the way forward. Even now, whenever I face difficulties, those words still ring in my ears: “If you keep something in mind, it will surely come true.” The ideal may be distant and uncertain, and everyone is afraid, but it doesn’t matter. As long as you keep it in mind, it will surely come true.
